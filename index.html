Example 2. Translation of Dai’s (1986, 67) explanation of Zhu’s calculations

Chi, the measurement of lengths, originates from the length of the pitch Huangzhong [typically assumed to be the pitch C], so the length of Huangzhong is one chi. Let one chi2 (that is, 100 cun2) [one chi equals ten cun in Chinese measurements] be the square of Huangzhong, then, according to the gougu theorem … the square of the hypotenuse would be 200 cun2. This value is at the same time the hypotenuse of the gougu square, the diameter of the square’s circumcircle, and the double of Ruibin [F♯]—we call that the length of the doubled Ruibin [F♯ below Huangzhong]. This is the value of the doubled Nanlü [A]. Multiplying the doubled Nanlü and two sides of 10 cun and then taking the cube root of this product, we get:

This is the value of the doubled Yingzhong [B]. The twelve tones begin with Huangzhong and ends with Yingzhong, cycling indefinitely. This is the truth of nature, as in the hexagrams of the I Ching … Therefore, to obtain the value of any tone of the twelve tones, one only needs to multiply the tone above [sic; should be below] it by 10 cun of Huangzhong, and then divide it by 10.59463… cun of the doubled Yingzhong, and the desired tone will be obtained. The same procedure applies to any of the other tones.

 


[1] The correspondence between Chinese and Western pitch names is often inconsistent. For example, instead of C, Dai (1986, 48) and Miao (1996, 98) list Huangzhong as F, and Huang (1986, 21) as D♯. Such discrepancies arise because Chinese music theory designates pitch standards with specific lengths, which vary according to the definition of the units (chi and cun) in any given era (Huang 1986, 19). The equivalence between Huangzhong and C (as assumed in Rehding [2022]) aids comprehension because Huangzhong is often taken as the first tone in the Chinese twelve-tone series (Miao 1996, 98).
